California Wildfires from Space

Explore California wildfire seasons from 2000–2024 using NASA MODIS satellite imagery and FIRMS active-fire detections. Each dot marks the mean centroid of all MODIS detections for a given year, sized by detection count and colored by Fire Radiative Power (FRP). Annual totals are aggregated from raw 1 km MODIS pixel data sourced from NASA FIRMS. Grey dots show all other years simultaneously for spatial comparison.

Write-up

Motivation

This visualization explores how California wildfire seasons evolved between 2000 and 2024 using NASA MODIS satellite imagery and FIRMS active-fire detections. By combining real satellite imagery with temporal fire statistics, we aim to help viewers discover spatial and temporal patterns in where and when California's most destructive fire seasons have occurred — patterns that are difficult to perceive from tabular data or static charts alone.

Design Rationale

We chose a satellite map as the primary canvas because wildfires are fundamentally spatial events — their geographic distribution across California's diverse terrain is central to understanding their impact. The selected year's fire activity is represented as a circle positioned at the mean centroid of all MODIS detections for that year, sized by total detection count using a square-root scale (which prevents the highest-detection years from dominating at the expense of others) and colored on a sequential yellow-to-red scale encoding Fire Radiative Power intensity. All other years are shown simultaneously as smaller grey dots, allowing viewers to compare the spatial shift in fire activity across years at a glance.

For temporal exploration, the year slider allows users to scrub through 25 years of fire history and watch the satellite imagery update in real time. The MODIS layer selector lets users switch between true color (natural appearance) and false color (Bands 7-2-1), which is particularly revealing for identifying burn scars — charred areas appear dark red-brown against healthy green vegetation. This combination of interactions enables discoveries that no static plot could support: for example, how the centroid of fire activity has shifted northward in recent severe years.

The accompanying bar chart serves a dual purpose: it provides an annual overview of detection intensity and acts as a navigation tool — clicking any bar jumps the map directly to that year. Likewise, clicking any grey dot on the map jumps to that year, creating two synchronized pathways into the data. Hover tooltips on both the fire dots and bar chart provide on-demand detail without cluttering the primary view. The pulse animation on the selected year's dot draws the viewer's eye and invites interaction.

Data Transformations

Annual MODIS active-fire detection counts were aggregated from raw NASA FIRMS CSV exports (one row per 1 km detection pixel). For each calendar year we computed: total detection count, cumulative Fire Radiative Power (FRP) in megawatts, and the peak single-day detection date and count. The centroid lat/lon displayed on the map represents the mean geographic position of all detections for that year, providing a summary location for the year's overall fire activity. These transformations are described in the subtitle and info panel so viewers understand what the dot's position and size represent.

Development Process

Team EPE consisted of three members: Edison, Erdene, and Prabhjas. Work was divided as follows:

Edison led data acquisition and preprocessing — writing Python scripts to download and aggregate raw FIRMS MODIS detection CSVs from NASA's archive, computing per-year summary statistics (total detections, cumulative FRP, peak-day detection date and count, and mean centroid coordinates). Edison also implemented the D3 bar chart, including click-to-navigate interaction and year-synchronized bar highlighting.

Erdene handled MODIS tile integration via the NASA GIBS REST API — identifying the correct WMTS tile coordinate ranges to cover California at zoom level 6, implementing layer switching between Terra and Aqua true and false color imagery, and managing tile error fallbacks between satellites when a layer was unavailable for a given date. Erdene also designed the overall page layout, HTML structure, and info panel.

Prabhjas built the fire dot visualization system — projecting centroid coordinates using D3's geoMercator, implementing zoom and pan behavior that keeps dots spatially aligned with the tile layer across all transform states, designing the radius and color scales, building the map legend, adding the multi-year historical dot layer with click navigation, and implementing the pulse animation and hover tooltips. Prabhjas also managed GitHub Pages deployment and CSS styling.

We estimate approximately 45 person-hours of total development time. The most time-consuming aspect was aligning the GIBS tile coordinate system (WMTS GoogleMapsCompatible tiles in EPSG:3857) with D3's Mercator projection, which required careful calibration of tile x/y index ranges and projection scale parameters to ensure the fire centroid dot landed in a geographically accurate position relative to the underlying satellite imagery. Processing and cleaning the raw FIRMS CSV data — which contains millions of individual 1 km detection pixels per year — and maintaining zoom transform state consistency across the tile layer and SVG dot layer were the other significant technical challenges.
